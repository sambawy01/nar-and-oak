Our Story

Two Fires. One Table.

Five thousand years before Texas lit its first pit, Egyptians were already slow-roasting whole oxen in earthen pits along the Nile — seasoned with cumin and black seed so sacred they were buried with pharaohs. Centuries later, German immigrants in central Texas threw cheap brisket into cotton gin smokers and accidentally created America's most revered barbecue tradition. Nār & Oak is where these two fire cultures finally meet.

The Nār

نار — Nār

Nār (نار) — Arabic for fire, but to Egyptians it means life. From Cairo's back-alley kofta meshweya over mangal charcoal to the Bedouin zarb — meat buried in desert sand over burning embers — Egyptian fire cooking is raw, primal, and unapologetic. We honour that with open flame, house-ground spices, and the smoky kiss of real charcoal.

The Oak

Oak — بلوط

In Texas, barbecue is religion and post oak is the holy wood. Pitmasters in Lockhart discovered this dense hardwood transforms a 6-kilo brisket over 14 patient hours into something spiritual. No sauce — just salt, pepper, and time. We bring that same discipline to the Red Sea: low heat, thick smoke, and the patience the desert already taught us.

The result is Brisket dusted with Dukkah, Burnt Ends glazed in Tamarind, Pulled Beef Shawerma, Smoked Hawawshi... You've had BBQ. You haven't had this.
